Purpose

Tennis swings are extremely technical, as good technique is key to finding the balance of power and finesse required to play well. Traditionally, much of swing analysis has been done by eye. Coaches stand off to the side and watch the swing’s smoothness, the contact point, and follow through. Additionally, players tend to adjust their swing based on the feel of their previous swings. Advanced players may use assistive technology to help improve their technique further, such as slow motion cameras. This process is slow, prone to human error, and requires a skilled coach. This kind of training can be prohibitively expensive, with private lessons often reaching over $100/hour (James).

That’s why we built the Forehand Fixing Tool.

The purpose of this project is to automatically analyze tennis swings based on IMU data taken from the tennis player’s arm. Using hardware nearly every player already owns and an inexpensive mount, we can provide coaching in real time for free. We are looking for two key insights: swing power and swing smoothness. Swing power helps determine the strength of the hit, and swing smoothness helps determine both the efficiency of the swing.

Demo Setup

For this prototype, we attached a cell phone to one of our team members’ upper arms. The arm used was the one doing the forehand motion, (traditionally) with the dominant hand. We then recorded data while that team member hit five forehands. 

Without going into too much detail, forehands have these distinct parts: the backswing, contact, and follow-through. Players may opt for more topspin (created upwards from contact with racket, bounces higher) or a flat (for speed, bounces low) stroke, but these are not generated directly from the upper arm (USTA).

Our proof of concept meets our goals of delivering affordable, automated analysis by using a smartphone and readily available armband. We used a smartphone collecting acceleration and orientation data through the MATLAB mobile app. Although the placement is not ideal from an analysis perspective, a smartphone is too heavy for a player to wear on their forearm without negatively impacting their swing. As a result, we decided to improve our algorithm to deal with this challenge to keep the user experience optimal. For the sake of our proof-of-concept, it was necessary to manually identify the time windows for each swing. However, we believe it would be possible to determine this programmatically with further research.

Motion Model

Before collecting our data, we considered the potential analysis we could perform and how the motion would be captured. We realized that if we wanted to capture a full swing without forcing the player to modify their swing, Our only option was to collect 3 axes of acceleration and 3 axes of orientation data. We had an understanding of how the motion occurs in 3D space, which makes more sense than the motion relative to the smartphone. This meant that we would have to change coordinate spaces so the orientation data was a must. This also informed our mounting solution. Although we initially hoped to capture data from the forearm, our priorities were reducing impact on the player and preventing rotation due to slippage.

Based on watching each other swing  without the sensor a few times, We expected the swing to exist in the 0.5 Hz range. We also observed that it was a somewhat looping motion which made us optimistic there would be clear patterns in the frequency domain. Our key insight comparing swings was that less confident swings would often pause or adjust throughout. This is why we expected a smooth FFT to correspond to a clean, smooth swing.

Other aspects of the swing we expected to see in the velocity and acceleration data were spinand power. We understood that topspin is created by the upward motion of the racket, and saw this in action comparing hits with and without heavy spin. Power, unsurprisingly would be linked to the speed of the racket at impact. 

This initial analysis drove our design for the Shot Score and selection of subscores. However, after collecting data we realized we had committed a major source of error: the players steps. Based on the footage, we observed this to occur in even lower frequencies than the swing. We updated our analysis to include a high pass filter, and that that it successfully removed the issues we were experiencing.

Raw Data

The raw data has a few clear features, but is not ready for any useful analysis. Attempting to calculate position based on this data results in confusing and outlandish results. Because shot pacing is not perfectly consistent, the FFT of this data reveals nothing of value.

Single Shot

For analysis, we isolate our data to a single shot at a time. We can start to infer some features (backswing, contact, follow through) based on intuition but that data is still noisy.

Determining Global Coordinates

No matter where you mount the sensors, a good tennis swing requires rotation. As we discussed in our motion model, this makes it impossible to fully understand our motion based on acceleration data alone. Luckily, any smartphone or IMU sold today has sensors capable of measuring the device’s orientation. 

To account for orientation changes, our algorithm transforms the acceleration data into the global coordinate frame. To do this, at each timestep we construct a 3D rotation matrix based on the orientation sensor data. In reality, the orientation sensor is a virtual sensor that calculates the device's orientation based on sensor fusion of different IMU sensors (Google). This is valuable because it results in the device’s orientation being reported relative to the gravity vector, so it can be used to find the global coordinate system(). Then, we are able to transform the acceleration vectors from the phone’s coordinate system to the global coordinate system using matrix multiplication.

Processing the Signal

At this point, our data starts to be clearer. Before, the acceleration data mostly looked the same along each axis. While there were noticeable peaks, there was not much else identifiable.

After transforming to the global coordinate system, the different axes begin to look more distinguishable. This is what we would expect because the player's arm motion is not the same in every axis.

However, there are still two misleading aspects of our data. Even in this drill, players have a tendency to drift slightly from their starting position. There is also a constant acceleration due to gravity. To isolate the motion of the swing, we want to remove these effects. There are many ways to accomplish this, ranging from the delightful simple to the unreasonably complex. Thankfully, the simple solution exists in the signal processing we have been learning.

From our motion model, we know that acceleration due to gravity is constant (frequency of 0Hz) and the player’s motion should be less than 0.25Hz while the swing is 0.5Hz or higher. 

We can remove these frequencies from our signal by transforming it from the time domain to the frequency domain using the DFT (Discrete Fourier Transform). In any good signal processing package, this can be accomplished with the FFT (Fast Fourier Transform), an efficient method for calculating the DFT of a signal.

With our signal in the frequency domain, we can filter out undesirable frequencies by reducing their amplitudes.

Then, we transform the signal back to the time domain with the inverse FFT to produce a filtered signal. This is crucial for motion reconstruction. Without this filtering our velocities will trend towards infinity and there is no noticeable trend.

Clean Swing

We use the orientation data to project from the phone's coordinate system into global coordinates. This is necessary because the phone is constantly rotating during a swing.

We also use the FFT and IFFT to apply an high pass filter which removes gravity and the player's whole body motion from the data.

From here, we are able to reconstruct the position by integrating and score the shot based on the frequency domain representation of the signal.

Building Swing Replay

To reconstruct the swing we first find the velocity in each direction. This is accomplished by numerically integrating the acceleration. Numeric integration of our discrete signal is accomplished by taking the trapezoidal area under each time interval and summing them together.

After integrating, we subtract the mean of the data to center it around zero. This prevents the next integral from drifting to infinity. This is a reasonable decision because the motion should return around the starting position (the "ready position" before the shot).

Finally, we can finish reconstructing the motion by integrating again with the same method to find the position of the phone in the global coordinate space.

Power Score

In order to evaluate the power score, we need to calculate the max speed at which the player’s arm was moving. When it comes to power in a swing, it’s not acceleration that matters but speed.
Throughout our subscores, we use non-linear scales to model the diminishing returns experienced as far as impact on the game with more powerful and spin heavy shots.

Smoothness Score: Good

We expect to see some peaks in the FFT of even a smooth swing. However, compared to a beginners swing the difference is night and day. To evaluate smoothness, we step through the frequency domain and count how many times there is a significant drop between two frequencies. Our smoothness algorithm finds only 1 "cliff" in this sample.

With this begginer's swing, our algorithm finds 10 "cliffs" and 9 "bumps" in this sample, resulting in a very low score. The swing has a greater presence of low frequencies, which makes sense for type of major shakiness it aims to penalize.

Rating Scale

Our scale and subscores were adjusted so that beginner players with improper form receive a D or below, with advanced players consistently receiving an A or above, while intermediate players receive a score somewhere in the middle.

Our Shot Score consists of multiple sub scores (from 0-10) which are averaged together. We report subscores instead of raw figures because players are unlikely to be able to understand the raw metrics as their connection to technique are not immediately clear. Because numeric scales can be hard to reason about, we report using the common letter scale.

Limitations

The biggest limitation to the prototype we assembled is the data available to us. Because we only had one accelerometer mounted to the upper arm, we aren’t capturing the movement of the elbow. While we originally proposed using this data to detect early causes of tennis elbow, we weren’t able to mount a sensor in a way to measure the motion that would cause tennis elbow (forearm movement), as our trials led to a lot of twisting and movement which were very difficult to secure. Another implication of our sensor placement is that we do not have a full picture of the motion of the racket. For most purposes the racket position can be determined from wrist position and orientation. However, this is not possible using the arm mount because the forearm can move independently of the upper arm.

We account for the fact that players are constantly moving by using a high pass filter to remove frequencies outside of the range of a swing (below 0.25 Hz). In drills like we studied, players are not rapidly repositioning. However, in a match or more complex drill, complex and rapid footwork is expected. This would potentially fall into the frequency range we are analyzing.

Next Steps

One solution to our issue of sensor placement would be to utilize a wearable device. Many athletes already have fitness trackers with the same sensor package found in a smartphone. A wearable device would be more comfortable and allow us to get data from the wrist for deeper analysis. Using a wearable device would also allow the app to collect video from the player's smartphone. This would allow them to see the sensor data overlaid with video of their swing to help them visualize the score.

We would also like to move beyond forehands. The same analysis could be applied to other shots as well as serves. Notably, Apple has recently been advertising the ability to perform a more primitive analysis of serves than what we present using the Apple Watch. For serves in particular, we believe the wrist mounted sensor would be necessary to fully understand the motion. Most of the movement in a serve is found in the rotation and snapping of the forearm; the upper arm is motionless during the peak of the serve.

To bring this concept to market, we would want to follow the route of using a wrist mounted smartwatch or fitness tracker combined with wireless sensors on the torso and upper arm with a smartphone recording video. The wireless sensors could be built using an IMU and a microcontroller with wireless capabilities for less than $10 in materialsallowing us to deliver a more complete solution without losing sight of our goal to deliver affordable, automated tennis coaching. This would allow us to get the best image of the players motion and fully recreate the motion of their dominant arm and body. It would also allow us to completely isolate the motion of the arm for our analysis. Additionally, we could perform analysis in real time using mobile processing instead of transferring the data to a PC.
